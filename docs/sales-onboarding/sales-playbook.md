# Sales Playbook

The full flow from cold prospect to closed deal. Follow this for every opportunity.

---

## The seven stages

1. **Source** — find the right businesses
2. **Outreach** — first contact
3. **Discovery call** — 30 minutes, structured
4. **Audit & proposal** — short, written, branded
5. **Demo / proposal review** — walk them through it
6. **Close** — agreement signed, deposit paid
7. **Handoff** — transfer to fulfillment, log the win

Average cycle: 7–14 days from first contact to signed agreement on small deals (Starter/Foundation), 14–30 days on larger ones (Pro/Accelerator).

---

## Stage 1: Source

### Where to find prospects

- **Google Business Profile gaps:** search "[service] near me" in a target city. Look for businesses with no website, an outdated site, fewer than 20 reviews, or no recent posts.
- **Yelp / industry directories:** same filter — outdated or thin profiles.
- **Cold drive-bys:** notice a local business with a hand-written sign or no website on their truck/storefront. Snap a photo of the truck and look them up.
- **Facebook groups & Nextdoor:** business owners asking for web help.
- **Referrals from existing clients:** highest close rate. Always ask after a successful project.
- **Industry-specific:** Katzkin dealers, LLUMAR installers, ServiceTitan users, etc. Lists are public and these businesses already invest in their operations.

### Qualification — quick gut check before reaching out

A prospect is worth pursuing if **at least three** of these are true:

- They've been in business 3+ years
- They have a physical address (not a pure side hustle)
- Their website is missing, outdated, or visibly broken
- They have employees (not a solo operator nights/weekends)
- They list services on a directory like Yelp/Angi/HomeAdvisor
- They run any kind of marketing (FB page, GBP, paid ads)

If they don't meet three, move on. We don't sell to businesses that aren't ready to invest.

## Stage 2: Outreach

### Email template — cold (use sparingly)

Use this only when you've actually looked at their site and can name a specific gap.

> Subject: a few small things I noticed on your site
>
> Hi [name],
>
> I run a small web and marketing shop here in Austin and came across [their site] while looking at [their industry] in the area. [One specific compliment about their business — gallery, reviews, location, anything real.]
>
> A few small things I noticed that I think would actually pay off without a full rebuild:
>
> - **[Specific gap 1]** — [one sentence on why it matters]
> - **[Specific gap 2]** — [one sentence]
> - **[Specific gap 3]** — [one sentence]
>
> Happy to put together a quick audit with screenshots if you want a closer look. No obligation, just thought it was worth flagging.
>
> Best,
> [your name]
> Polaris Point | polarispoint.io

**Rules for the cold email:**
- One email. Never blast a list with the same message.
- The "specific gap" must be real. No "boost your SEO" generic openers.
- Skip subject lines like "Quick question" or anything with a fake-curious tone.
- Don't pitch a price in the first email. The goal is a 15-minute call.

### Phone / walk-in — local outreach

If you're in the area, walking in works better than email for trades and food service. Bring a printed audit page (one sheet, branded). Ask for the owner. If they're not in, leave the sheet with a card.

### Follow-up cadence

- Day 0: initial email
- Day 4: short follow-up ("circling back — happy to send the audit if useful")
- Day 11: third and final touch ("closing this loop, let me know if it's ever useful")

After three touches, drop them. Re-add to a quarterly nurture list, not a weekly one.

## Stage 3: Discovery call (30 minutes)

### The five questions

Drive the conversation. Don't let it drift into chitchat for 25 minutes.

1. **"Walk me through your business — how long, what you do, who your customers are."**
   *(Builds rapport, gives you context for the rest of the call.)*

2. **"How are most new customers finding you today?"**
   *(Reveals their current marketing reality. If "word of mouth," there's a huge opportunity. If "Google," ask what they're spending.)*

3. **"What's the biggest bottleneck right now — getting leads, converting them, or delivering the work?"**
   *(Focuses the pitch. We solve the first two.)*

4. **"If we fixed that, what would it be worth to you over a year?"**
   *(Gets them thinking in revenue, not cost. Anchors the conversation away from "how much is a website.")*

5. **"What's stopped you from doing this before?"**
   *(Surfaces objections early — bad past agency experience, budget concerns, time, internal politics.)*

### What to capture

- Business name, owner name, phone, email
- Years in business, # of employees, service area
- Current website URL (or note "none")
- Current monthly marketing spend
- Top 1–2 problems in their words
- Decision-maker — is this person it, or do they need to loop someone in?
- Timeline — "next month," "next quarter," "no rush"
- Budget signal — even a vague one

Log all of this in the CRM before the call ends.

### Closing the discovery call

> "Here's what I'd suggest. Let me put together a short audit and a proposal — three options at different price points so you can see what fits. I'll send it over by [day], and we'll book 20 minutes to walk through it. Sound good?"

Always book the next meeting before hanging up. A proposal sent without a scheduled review meeting closes 3x less often.

## Stage 4: Audit & proposal

### The audit (1 page)

Pulled from a template. Branded Polaris Point. Sections:

1. **What's working** — two or three real positives. Establishes you actually looked.
2. **What's costing you customers** — three to five specific gaps with screenshots.
3. **What we'd recommend** — your bundle pick, one sentence on why.
4. **What it costs** — the bundle price.
5. **What happens next** — signature line + deposit link.

Keep it short. One page, max two. Long proposals close less often.

### The proposal — three options

Always present three tiers, even if you know which one they need. The middle one is what most clients pick.

- **Option A:** the cheapest viable solution (often Starter or Foundation)
- **Option B:** what you actually recommend (usually Momentum)
- **Option C:** the premium build (Accelerator or custom)

Price each clearly. Include the monthly recurring next to the setup. No hidden fees.

### Send-and-schedule rule

Send the proposal 24 hours before the review meeting. Do not send it without a meeting on the calendar.

---

## Stage 5: Demo / proposal review (20–30 minutes)

### Walk-through structure

1. **Recap what they told you** (2 min) — "Last time you said the biggest issue was X. Here's what we'd do."
2. **Show a live demo site** in their vertical (5 min) — pull up the matching demo (plumber, salon, real estate, etc.) and click through it.
3. **Walk the proposal** (10 min) — what's in each option, what's not.
4. **Ask for the decision** (5 min) — "Which option feels right?"
5. **Handle objections** (5 min) — see objection bank below.

### Live demo tips

- Always share screen. Don't just describe.
- Open one of our existing demos (polarispoint.io/plumber, /salon, /real-estate, etc.) — pick the one closest to their industry.
- Show the mobile view. Most of their customers are on phones.
- If selling Beacon, log into a demo Beacon workspace and show real outputs — social posts, review responses, ad copy.

### Asking for the close

> "Which of the three feels right for where you are?"

Then **stop talking.** Silence is uncomfortable. Most people fill it with the answer. Don't rescue them with more options or more discounting.

---

## Stage 6: Close

### What signs

- Service agreement (DocuSign template — get from Pete)
- 50% deposit on setup fee, invoiced at signing (use the agreed payment method on file with Pete — do not generate your own payment links)
- First month of recurring billed on launch day, not signing day

### What to send within 1 hour of signing

1. Welcome email (template in CRM)
2. Onboarding form (Google Form — collects domain access, photos, copy, brand assets, GBP login, social logins)
3. Calendar invite for kickoff call (within 5 business days)

### What NOT to do

- Don't promise a launch date you didn't clear with fulfillment
- Don't promise specific features outside the tier
- Don't promise SEO rankings or ad ROI in writing
- Don't accept a verbal commitment as closed — only signed + deposited counts

---

## Stage 7: Handoff

### The handoff doc

Before fulfillment starts, write a one-page handoff in the CRM with:

- Client name, primary contact, phone, email
- Tier sold + monthly recurring
- Deposit paid (date, amount)
- Timeline promised
- Any custom commitments
- Notes from discovery: voice, brand, things to avoid
- Login info collected so far

### After handoff

- Stay on the welcome email thread until launch
- Attend the kickoff call (5 min, just to introduce fulfillment)
- Stop scoping new work. New requests go through fulfillment or back to you for an upsell quote.

### Sale credit

Sale is logged when:
- Agreement signed
- Deposit cleared
- Handoff doc complete

Until those three things happen, the sale is in pipeline, not in your commission count.

## Objection bank

The eight you'll hear most. Memorize these.

### "It's too expensive."

> "I get that. Most of our clients felt that way at first too. The way to think about it: if one new customer a month covers the whole investment, what's the math on that for your business?"

Then wait. Make them do the math out loud.

### "I already have a website."

> "Got it. The reason I asked is — when's the last time it brought you a real lead? Most of the sites I see still get traffic but don't convert. Want me to send a free 15-minute audit and you can decide if there's anything worth doing?"

### "I need to think about it."

> "Of course. What specifically do you want to think through? Sometimes I can answer it now, sometimes it's a real reason to wait. Either way I want to make sure you've got what you need."

### "Send me some info."

This is usually a soft no. Don't accept it.

> "Happy to. Quick question first — is now actually a bad time, or are you trying to politely tell me it's not for you? Either is fine, I just don't want to clutter your inbox if it's the second one."

### "We don't have time for this right now."

> "That's exactly why most of our clients hire us — they don't have time to do it themselves. The whole point is we run it, you don't. Let me show you what your involvement actually looks like — it's about 90 minutes total over two weeks."

### "We tried an agency before and it didn't work."

> "What happened?" *(Listen.)* "Yeah, that's a common story. Here's what's different about how we work: [specific differentiator that addresses what they said]. Can I show you the process?"

### "Can you guarantee leads / rankings / ROI?"

> "No, and I wouldn't trust anyone who does — Google has rules against it. What I can guarantee is the work itself: the site goes live in two weeks, the SEO is set up correctly, the content is on schedule. The leads and rankings follow when those are right."

### "Let me talk to my [partner / spouse / accountant]."

> "Totally — when can the three of us hop on a 15-minute call together? I'd rather answer their questions live than have you try to relay it."

If they refuse a three-way call, you don't have a real decision-maker. Mark the deal as long-cycle.

---

## What good looks like

After 90 days a salesperson should be running:

- **20–30 outreach touches per week** (cold + warm)
- **5–8 discovery calls per week**
- **2–3 proposals sent per week**
- **1–2 closes per week** (across tiers)

If you're under those numbers, the bottleneck is usually outreach volume — not closing skill.
